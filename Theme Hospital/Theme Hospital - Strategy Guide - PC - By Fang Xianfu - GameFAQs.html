Theme Hospital is an old game and has the difficulty to match; it's easy to lose on the harder difficulties and later levels without careful planning right from the start. This guide focuses on strategies for getting through the hardest levels of Theme Hospital with the least amount of panic-induced baldness. The hair restorer doesn't exist in real life - use this guide instead!

The guide is in three main sections: the strategies and general principles of how to build a hospital that will run well; tactics, tips and tricks that will help you get a bit more out of your hospital; and a brief look at an example hospital from near the end of the game.

Building a winning hospital

These are fairly advanced strategies and you won't really need them on the early levels. Use the first couple of levels to get used to how the game works. Level 2 introduces research. Level 3 introduces consultants, when you can start using the consultant GP strategy. Level 4 introduces the training room, when you can start using that room to really break the game.

The training room is the most important room in the game

This isn't an exaggeration, and the reason why is simple: consultants are vital to the smooth running of your hospital (see the next section). The fewer non-consultants you have to babysit to keep them away from anything important, the better.

Hiring consultants is very expensive (around $300 a month) but juniors can be hired for around $100 and trained up relatively quickly. In addition, after a couple of years on the later levels, juniors will be the only staff available to hire, so you'll need to have already set up a way to train staff for the late-game.

By the end of the second year, you ideally want to have fired anyone who isn't a consultant or on the way there.

Creating the perfect teacher

The training room of your dreams needs the perfect doctor to teach in it. This guy will be a consultant with all three specialisms - this saves you headaches having to juggle different teachers in and out of the room when you have a shortage of  one kind of specialist. All his future students will be triple-specialists as well, which breaks the game fairly well.

Once a doctor becomes a consultant, they're kicked out of the training room forever, so you have to teach the doctor all three specialisms before they reach that point. How many skills they can pick up before they're thrown out is determined by how full the green Abacus-vs-Computer bar is when they start training - the fuller it is, the less time they have to develop their specialisms before they're kicked out. A normal mid-level doctor can usually learn specialisms from one consultant, and a junior can usually learn from two different consultants.

Getting all three skills sounds like a tall order, but it's actually pretty simple to pull off on most levels. Until you're happy you're on the right track, put the game onto the slowest speed on the first of every month and check out the new staff available. Work out what you're missing, and hire to fill the gaps. Before the end of the first summer, you should have your plan in place.

There are lots of different paths to the perfect teacher. You can sometimes get lucky and hire a mid-level doctor with all three specialisms, and train him up. Sometimes you can get a junior with one specialism and a consultant with the other two. Sometimes you can get two consultants with all three specialisms between them, and train up a green junior. You get the idea.

If you simply can't get all three up fast, surgery is the most important. You need two surgeons per operation and in the late game you'll often have two running at once, perhaps even three. Research is the least important because it's not necessary immediately, but starting weak on research will cripple you in the late-game.

...or cheat a bit

The doctors available change each month, but they aren't random - the names and descriptions can change, but their skills are the same every playthrough.

Just start the game on highest speed, slow it down to slowest at the start of the month and see who you'll get. Which staff are available depends on how many are in the pool when the month ends, so it can be helpful to hire useless doctors and then immediately sack them to free up space in the pool, if you're struggling to find the doctors you need.

Keep going until you've worked out how you're going to get your perfect teacher, then re-start and execute the plan. You can usually get all the staff you need by July of the first year, even on the worst levels.

Optimise your training room

Juniors walk really slowly. The teacher does as well while he's in the training room. Optimising the room can have a big impact on training speed as a result.


A decently optimal training room, with adjacent staff room

There are four things you need to do to optimise a training room:

  1. Keep the lecture chairs and the projector as close as you can to the door so people can get out fast.
  2. Have the skeleton(s) as close as possible to the projector - the teacher sometimes walks over to the skeleton to point at it and will have to walk out of the room from there instead. He never uses bookcases so put those wherever.
  3. Build a staff room as close as possible to the training room - ideally the doors should be one tile apart to keep down-time to a minimum. Juniors walk so slowly, you don't want them having to trek across the whole hospital to rest up. You may need to build a second, smaller staff-room next to your training room to accomplish this.
  4. Fill it up with junk. The value of the room affects training to some degree, so as you can afford it, fill the room with bookcases (which add value but don't affect the teacher's animations) to improve training speed.

The ideal training room will have three or four lecture chairs, a projector, and a skeleton at the door end, and 7-9 bookcases at the far end, with a staff room next door.

Keep it full

Once you've got your perfect teacher and your perfect room, your next step is simple - keep it full of students! Juniors are cheap, so every time one becomes a consultant, hire another one even if you're not sure you need to. You will soon.

A final note about happiness

Doctors you've trained yourself will be paid pennies even if you've given them all three specialisms and trained them to consultant level. However, if they ever get unhappy enough to demand a pay rise, their demands will be outrageous - I've seen as much as $750 demanded. Whether or not you sack them after that demand is up to you (one doctor on $750 and a bunch on $100 is still cheaper than loads on $300), but try to avoid this by keeping your doctors happy in the first place.

The GP's office is important too

The GP's office is the most important room a patient will visit in your hospital. Over a whole game, your GP's offices will see hundreds of patients up to twice each. I can't overstate how important it is to keep your GP's offices running smoothly. There are two steps to making this happen:

Consultants only!

Once they're available, a non-consultant should never set foot inside a GP's office under any circumstances. There are two reasons for this - the first is simply that consultants walk faster and will see more patients as a result.

The second, far more important, reason is that patients receive an initial boost to their diagnosis meter from the GP, and this boost is determined by the doctor's skill level. A consultant in the GP's office will mean that many patients get a diagnosis immediately and go straight to treatment, which completely bypasses diagnosis rooms and another GP visit.  Even those that are not diagnosed immediately will need to visit fewer rooms to get there. This reduces the load on your other doctors significantly, gets patients out of the hospital faster, and gets their money into your wallet sooner. You can then focus on building and researching cure rooms, which earn you lots of money, rather than diagnosis rooms.

This is why the training room is so important - early on, before you train your own staff, you'll have to babysit your non-consultants to keep them out of the room, and on some levels even swap out your teacher when your only other consultant goes on break. You want to sack everyone who isn't a consultant as quickly as possible.

Optimise the room

A GP's office might see as many as 500 patients over the course of a game, so of all the rooms, GP's offices benefit the most from optimisation. That extra tile to the patient chair might seem small, but over 500 visits that time will add up.

Thankfully, the furniture in the room is simple, so it's easy to optimise:


  1. Desk and filing cabinet next to each other, with the desk facing northeast or northwest. You'll see that there's a space on one side of the chair that is highlighted as part of the desk, and one space that is free for putting other objects in. The space you're not allowed to place things in is the space that the doctor will move to when he stands up from the desk, and the space in front of the filing cabinet should always touch this space. Sometimes between patients, the doctor will stand up to put something in the cabinet, and keeping walking to a minimum stops patients waiting. Putting the filing cabinet on the wrong side will increase the walking distance by 500%, so don't get them mixed up. If the desk is facing south, for some reason the doctor will always stand up on one side and sit down on the other, making south-facing desks much less-optimal than north-facing ones.
  2. Patient chair next to the door. Patients are slow, and having them walk all over the room is a waste of time. The chair should always go right next to the door.

Some optimal GP's offices

 

Don't skimp on Research

You need a research room on every level that it's available. Research will give you diagnosis rooms, clinics, drug improvements, and improve the durability of your machines.

If you've got consultants in your GP's offices, diagnosis rooms will be slightly less important, but you still need to research at least the X-ray and Scanner. Clinics are important to research as well so you can earn more money by curing more patients. Machine improvements and drugs are of secondary priority. Drug improvements are mostly important on hard mode, where drugs start out less effective and thus kill patients more often.

As the levels go on, you'll need as big a room as possible. The number and skill level of the doctors in the room determines how fast you research, and the number of doctors is determined by the number of desks - on later levels, you'll want as many as 6-8 desks in the room. The research room is perhaps the only exception to the "sack all non-consultants" rule, because they never do anything except research so they don't need micromanaging. If all you can get to start the game is a middle-of-the-road doctor, then go with it.

Since you're building a staff room next to your training room, it can be a good idea to build the research room in the same place. Staff from research and training never go anywhere except the staff room, so this group of three rooms can be way off in a corner somewhere if you need the space.

Laying out your hospital

Laying out your hospital is a pretty simple matter if you keep a few principles in mind.

Start small and grow

At the start of a level, your hospital doesn't need much. A staff area with a research room, training room (to start working on your teacher immediately!) and a staff room; a GP's office; a pharmacy; a psychiatric room; and a toilet are pretty much all you need to kick off the level, provided you have a consultant to staff the GP's office.

Most ailments at the start of a level are psychiatric or pharmaceutical cures, so you'll have those covered. There's no point in adding clinics until you get your first patient with that disease. And for those patients that the consultant GP can't diagnose first try, the psychiatry doubles as a diagnosis room.

Keep these rooms clustered together to begin with - they can even share a waiting area - and only add on to them as you need to. Have a plan for where you're going to put new rooms. The staff rooms can be off in a corner if space is an issue. It might even be worth buying a plot for them out of the way, especially if it saves you from having to move them later.

Separate the hospital into zones

Patients follow three basic steps in their trip through your hospital:

  1. First consultation
  2. Additional diagnosis and another GP visit (this step can be skipped if the initial GP diagnoses them straight away)
  3. Treatment

This is a pretty clear invitation to lay out your hospital in four zones - one for each step, and one for the staff-only rooms.

First consultation

This area should be at the main entrance to the hospital. You'll need one or two reception desks, a number of GP's offices - one or two on the first few levels, as many as four on the last level - a toilet, and a waiting area. This area should have easy access to the treatment rooms (where many patients will go immediately) and the diagnosis area.

Diagnosis

The diagnosis area should house your diagnosis rooms and one GP's office. Once patients are diagnosed, they visit the GP again, and an office here can stop them trekking back to the first consultation area.

Most of the diagnosis rooms are total garbage, so don't pack this area full of rooms unless queues are forming. You'll need a ward and a psychiatry anyway to treat diseases. The X-ray and Scanner are good, the Ultrascan is okay, and everything else can be skipped, though you might need a General Diagnosis or something until those rooms are researched. On earlier levels, just build whatever is available if you're getting patients that can't be diagnosed.

Treatment

These rooms need to be very accessible - patients need to come here from first consultation, diagnosis, and the helipad. It's usually best to cluster them around the helipad as much as possible, to keep emergency patients from walking too far. They needn't all be in the same area, either - clinics especially can be isolated, since they only cure one type of patient each and don't often have queues.

One extra consideration is the psychiatry and the ward, which straddle between diagnosis and treatment. It's usually best to put the diagnosis area off on its own, with the ward and psychiatry in between that area and the operating theatre. This part is hard to get right, so don't worry too much if ward patients end up going to the wrong GP.

Staff

This area can be way off on its own, as patients never need to come here (except to die!). Putting your research, training, and staff rooms together in their own block can let you make those rooms big without worrying about fitting other things around them.

Have a plan

It's fine to juggle rooms around a lot - you'll have to, because space is always at a premium - but always have a plan for how you're going to end up with nicely-divided zones as time goes on. You don't want patients walking miles between diagnosis rooms, then back to a GP, before trekking off to treatment in another time zone.

Tips for getting more out of your hospital

Now that you've got a hospital staffed by well-trained doctors, with efficient GPs, and laid out well, there are a few more little things you can do to improve your hospital's running.

Abuse the game speed controls

Set the game to slowest speed as soon as the level starts to give yourself plenty of build time. Any time anything is going wrong - an emergency when you haven't got the clinic built, or an earthquake - or you're feeling rushed, flip the game into slowest. This takes the stress out of these situations and stops patients from getting lost.

Build extra rooms

Your most frequently-used rooms can often end up with big queues. These are rooms like GPs, pharmacies, operating theatres, and psychiatries. It can also applies to clinics that suffer from emergencies. You can save yourself some headaches by building a second room and turning it off by setting its queue size to 0 by right-clicking on the room's door. The room won't be used until the queue for the first room is full, after which patients will go there automatically.

I typically have three or four pharmacies with two open, two operating theatres (with the open one set to only have a queue of 2 or 3), and two or three psychiatries in later levels. This also helps you clear through emergencies.

In my initial consultation area, I frequently build two GP's offices and close one of them right at the start of the game to help with rushes.

Optimising corridors

Try to keep all corridors at least two spaces wide to allow plenty of traffic through. Benches and drinks machines block pathing, so don't build them on both sides of a 3-tile corridor.

If you arrange your rooms carefully, you can create natural waiting areas off the main paths through the hospital. This keeps the corridors free and supposedly helps to stop the spread of epidemics.

Optimising rooms

Beyond the GP's office and the training room, which have their own sections, completely optimising the room isn't vital to winning. You should at least think about the path a patient is going to take through the room and try not to make it horrible, but most rooms don't see enough traffic to make a difference.

The one exception to this is the psychiatry. Trying to fit the changing screen into this room can be a real challenge sometimes and it can end up in a weird place. If the route is very long, this can cause you problems with King Syndrome emergencies. These patients go from the door to the couch, then behind the screen, then back out again. If this takes a long time, you can easily fail an emergency.

There is one other optimisation for psychiatry, too - the psychiatrist often stands up to take a book from a bookshelf, so keep them close to the chair as well.

Applying these techniques - Croaking Hospital

Croaking is the penultimate level of the game, and I thought it'd be useful to show these techniques in practice.


 

In this picture, Purple is my Initial Consultation area, Orange is my Diagnosis area, Green is my Treatment area (the helipad is just to the south of the staff area), and Blue is the Staff area. The rooms labeled "C" are clinics.

You can see that the green and orange areas have plenty of room to expand as demands on my hospital grow. At the start of the game, the green, orange and purple zones were all squashed into in the middle area of the hospital, but I've pushed them out as the level has gone on.

Silly-looking dead ends are waiting areas to leave the corridors free for walking, and each block has its own toilets.

This level is quite convenient because there's an obvious place for the staff rooms. I bought this block right at the start of the game and put my staff rooms in their permanent places on day 1. This central location is great, because staff can get there easily from anywhere on the map to rest up.

I could improve this hospital by spreading the clinics out into the new space - they're really cramped - and by adding more GP's offices to the initial consultation area.

Another obvious improvement is moving the psychiatry - it's a diagnosis room, and patients diagnosed there will go back to the initial consult area rather than to the dedicated GP in the diagnosis area. Moving it up towards the ward - perhaps by moving one of the existing diagnosis rooms into the open space - will help improve that and open up more space for clinics.
