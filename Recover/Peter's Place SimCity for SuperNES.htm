Foreword
With some excpetions in controls, graphics and music, SimCity is the exact same game both on the SuperNES and on a personal computer such as an IBM-compatible or Macintosh. Because of the release of SimCity 2000, however, there has been some confusion regarding the names of the games. Because SNES games are never updated (ie. there will never be a Super Mario World version 1.1), the SNES version still is, and always will be, called "SimCity." However, since computer games are often updated and re-released, "SimCity" is the same game as "SimCity Classic." The name "SimCity Classic" was introduced to avoid confusion after the release of "SimCity 2000." 

Because the SNES and the IBM versions of "SimCity" are so similar, players or either version can benefit from this page for strategy and tips. However, detailed technical info or controls (like cheats) shown here are only for use with the SNES version. They probably would have made the cheats the same for both games, but remember that the IBM version has the use of a 101-Key Keyboard and a mouse, while the SNES only has a 9-button Controller. 

For SC2K Players Taking "Flashbacks" - Don't forget that this game is much, much simpler than SC2K (SimCity 2000). Things like schools, hospitals, water and prisons either have no relevance to the game, or the game takes care of them automatically. However, the game is slightly more risky, since there's no way to lock out the disasters...

Choosing a Landform.  One of the most important decisions you'll have. Why?  Because less water means more land, and more land means more zones, and more zones mean more population.  In a letter, Nintendo told me that the best landforms are 61, 137 and 714.  On their site, they add that the first choice is 13% water, while the other 2 landforms have 15% water.  The bonus scenario Freeland has no water at all, but because it is a scenario, you will not receive any gifts.  Decide for yourself whether you can get more population with little water and all gifts or no water and no gifts.

Finding a copy of SimCity SNES
Most video games only last on the store shelves for about a year or two because few games are popular enough to demand a longer stay.  Since SimCity is over 5 years old, you probably won't be able to walk into your local game or electronics store and walk out with the game 5 minutes later - at least brand new anyway.  Here are some tips Nintendo provided to Peter's Place that can help you find SimCity or any other older game.
 
  • Start at the stores. If they don't have it on the shelf, ask if they can special order it. For a deposit (usually 50% of the retail price) you can have the game shipped to the store within a week or two.
  • Try mail order houses that might have a few extra copies lingering around. Here are some vendors that Nintendo suggests.  Note that some vendors don't have a toll free number.  Also, links to vendors do not necessarily mean you can order online - the links are just provided for those who want more info about the vendor.
    • Digital Stuff (NOA mentioned this in Sept. '96, but not in July '97)
      • USA (800)262-7462 - Toll Free
      • Canada (510)438-6688, ext. 104 - Toll Call 
    • The remaining numbers work in both Canada and the USA
      Electronics Botique (800) 800-0032 - Free
      Famcom (253) 927-1104 - Toll Call
      JC Penney (800) 222-6161 - Free
      Service Merchandise (800) 251-1212 - Free
      Spiegel (800) 345-4500 - Free
      VLM Entertainment (815) 434-3811 - Toll Call
  • If you are desperate, call the manufacturer. Although Maxis created SimCity, Nintendo actually produced the SNES version, so you should be able to buy the game direct from Nintendo of America (NOA). The catch is that they only sell refurbished copies. Refurbished copies are not new, but in good condition and they come with the same 90-day guarantee new games have. One other catch is that the game may not always be in stock. As of September 1996, NOA had a "good supply" with a price of $34.90 plus $2.50 S&H. Payment can be made by MC or Visa over the phone, or you can send a check or money order via snail mail. To buy from NOA, call (800)255-3700, toll free from the US and Canada. Be prepared to do a lot of button pressing before you can talk to a live person.  If you get fed up with this, try NOA's e-mail or web site.
  • Check the "used game rack" at your local software or electronics store. Although the profit margin is high, it is usually still lower than buying new. Make sure there's a warranty in case the game fails. (Try this only as a second to last resort.)
  • As a very last resort, try a video rental store. If they have a copy that hasn't been renting too much lately, they may sell it to you. This is the worst way, though, since there is usually no warranty, nor is there any way to tell how old the battery is (on savable games like SimCity, Super Mario World or Zelda).
    If you are really desparate, try using the Internet to track down people who would be willing to sell you the game.  There are many postings daily for people looking to buy/sell/trade games on newsgroups like rec.games.video.nintendo and I am sure that there are some e-mail groups out there with similar content.

    Tips & Tricks
    The Million Dollar Code!
    Well, actually, it'll only get you $999,999, but what's $1.00? Follow these steps CAREFULLY:
    1. Make sure you have some PD's, FD's and transportation expenses on the budget.
    2. Spend all your money and wait until December.
    3. When the tax screen comes up (in Dec) press and HOLD the L Button. Exit the tax screen and go back to it again.
    4. Now, raise all funding levels to 100%. Leave the tax screen.
    5. When you return to the regular city screen, let go of the L Button. Now, you should have $999,999.
    6. Repeat every few decades as necessary....
    Some tips to help you master this sometimes difficult code:
    • Practice this at the start of a new city. Build a few things to create expenses, then, spend the rest building power lines and bulldozing them. The key is to NOT start with a serious city. If the trick doesn't work, you'll have to wait until next year. If you have a city going without money, people will move out quickly.
    • Once the trick works, THEN you can start building seriously.
    • You can't have more than $999,999 at once. Don't waste your time doing it again until you're back to $0. It'll probably take a few decades.

    Time to Make the Donuts!
    One way to efficiently store people in your city is to make "Donut Blocks." Start by building a square of TRACK (I'll explain later) big enough to accomodate 9 zones, in a 3 by 3 layout. Next, build 8 zones, a mixture of R's and C's, along the outer edges of the zone, so that if forms a "donut".  The next step is to fill the middle of the donut with PD's or parks. When you get a gift, find out what area of the city the gift would do the most improving in, then place the gift right in the middle of a donut in that area. In other words, put your gifts near the slums so that theywon't be slums anymore. Don't put a gift next to a TOP, since TOPs are as good as it gets. For starters, you should be able to get 5 or 6 TOPs once the city has been running for awhile. More experienced players should be able to get 10 to 15. Really good experts (not us) can get more than that (you must, if you want to reach 500,000).

    Building Plan "B"
     Ben Reynolds, (slr@key-net.net) says that he disagrees with the Donut Block plan, saying that his own strategy has brought him population levels exceeding 550,000 people, which more than meets the mark for Megalopolis level.
    As seen in the graphic at right, Ben says he prefers to build columns which are two zones wide. The sides are surrounded with train tracks. In addition, Ben uses the classic strategy of placing all his Industrial zones on the edge of the city map. He also suggests that you try to fill your waterfront property with Commercial zoning, as it will do very well along the water. I agree totally with these final 3 points, but I cannot comment good or bad on the 2-column strategy in general as I have neither tried it nor seen it in action. We'll just have to take Ben's word for it, but I think it would work.

    Getting Rid of Problems
    Your city will usually have many problems at all times. Listed below is each problem and how to solve (or at least minimize) it. Although you will get more "YES" Votes (on the opinion screen, "Is the Mayor Doing a Good Job?") for solving big problems, you will also be thanked for getting rid of the small ones. Dr. Wright will offer advice for problems that reach 20% or more on the opinion screen.

    POLLUTION - You can never completely get rid of pollution, but you can help it greatly with 4 easy steps: 

  • Use nuclear power instead coal.  It costs more, but it causes no pollution.
  • Use rails instead of roads for the same reason.
  • Spread out I zones and keep them separated from the ports.  Clumping high polluters together is bad.
  • Build I zones along the edges of the city... half the pollution goes off the edges!
  • Fill in empty spaces with parks - it eats pollution and raises land value.
     


     
     
     
     
     
     
     
     

    HOUSING COSTS - Another one we can never get rid of. The best you can do is keep R's and C's close (use one of the zoning strategies above) and make sure there are zones available where land value is low, as well as high. I really think this is just a "default" problem the opinion screen lists when there is nothing else to complain about.

    CRIME - Place PD's so that they give overlapping coverage. You'll need a PD on almost every donut block (or every 8-10 tiles) as the population density rises. Look at the "Crime Rate" map to pinpoint crime areas, and build PD's there. Build parks in other open spaces... that raises land value and lowers crime. Don't be surprised if you eventually build over 100 PD's.

    TRAFFIC - You can't have traffic problems if you don't have roads, so build the entire city using mass transit.  It costs more, but it reduces pollution as well, so the pluses outweigh the minuses. In scenarios, use your spare time to bulldoze roads and replace them with rails.  If you don't have enough cash for this, at least straighten winding roads and eliminate excess roadways.
    UNEMPLOYMENT - To combat unemployment, you must always have zones that provide jobs.  At the beginning, start by building mostly R and I zones.  Try to put C zones in between the R's and I's.  Many sources say that the main job force should be made up from I zones, but as you approach 100,000 people, C zones should start gaining ground.  At 100,000, the C and I zones should be equal and after 100,000, you should be able to have more people working in C zones instead of I zones.  This even holds true in SimCity 2000.

    TAXES - The simplest way to end all complaints about taxes is to use the Million Dollar Code and lower the tax to 0%. When you run out of money (and you will) just redo the code.  If you prefer not to cheat, then start with a 7% tax rate.  Keep a well-balanced budget and once you have a comfortable nest egg, try lowering it to 4% or 5% to keep the people happy.  Nintendo says that if the land values are high, the people will be more willing to pay more taxes, but I say keep 'em as low as you can go.

    USING LANDFILLS EFFECTIVELY - As you run out of land, you will be able to have "Landfills" that will build a 3x3 section of land in any body of water.  You should have 3 or 4 of these by the time you run out of land.  I would suggest putting these together, so that you can build 2 zones next to each other.  The land value just being ON the water is high, so the land value of being IN the water will eventually allow any zones on a landfill to become a TOP, 90% of the time. 

    NO ROOM LEFT? - While SimCity SNES doesn't allow you to build your ports as big or small as you like, there is a way to downsize these facilities.  As a plane is flying over an airport, seaport or stadium, go to the disasters menu and choose Plane Crash.  The plane should instantly crash, taking out parts of the facility.  As long as one section of the facility survives, it still has a full effect on the city.  If you're good, you should be able to time it just right and have the plane take out precise sections of a building.  Just make sure you have an FD nearby!

    Pollution vs. Unemployment
    This is one of the biggest conflicts in the game because you can never get rid of both of these problems. If you have no pollution, you have no (or not enough) I zones, so there is Unemployment. To curb unemployment, you must build I zones, which cause pollution. All you can do is decide which problem is least harmful to your city and live with it.
     
    The Biggest Pain in the Neck
    Perhaps equally problematic is called the "Startup Bug." It's a bug because it isn't supposed to happen... and because this is a cartridge-based game, it will never be fixed, patched or corrected at all. When you turn the game on and open a previously saved city, the power goes out, pollution skyrockets and a crime spree starts while police and fire are inoperative. Although these problems last only until the game "warms up," they can do significant damage to your population. This happens because the game starts as soon as you load it. Instead of checking to see what's hooked up to power and where the police stations are before it starts, the game just checks these things during the first few months after you load it.

    I may have acidentally found a solution to this!  When you first load the game, just leave it on pause for about 2-3 minutes.  I did this once and was surprised to find that when I unpaused the game, everything was back to normal, as if the game was never stopped.  I think it's possible that the simulation engine just needs a few minutes to "warm up" - regardless of whether the game is paused or not.

    How to Beat the Scenarios
    In any scenario, even in SimCity 2000, there is a basic procedure that will lead to success.
  • First, solve the scenario's featured attraction as quickly and efficiently as possible.  Bulldoze around fires to prevent spreading and rebuild power lines before anything else to prevent major outages.
  • After the main problem is solved (or in between phases of the disaster for LasVegas) try to make other general improvements to the city.  Just look at the opinion screen, see what problems need to be solved and take care of them as described in the previous section.  Most likely, your main problems will be traffic, pollution and unemployment.
  • Keep in mind that you only have 2 save files available.  If you can't play the entire scenario in one sitting, you will need to use one of your 2 valuable slots to continue later... and you'll have to deal with the startup bug.
     


     
     
     
     
     
     
     
     

    And now some strategies custom-tailored for each scenario...
    BOSTON - In my opinion, this is the easiest scenario. Just bulldoze around the contaminated areas. Rebuild power lines, rails (even if roads were there originally) and build zones where space allows. Use the rest of the time to eliminate other problems as listed on the opinion survey.
    According to RJensen192@aol.com, "you can avoid the nuclear meltdown by pushing and holding the L button and then destroy the three nuclear power plants."  I have not tried this, but if anyone out there can verify, let me know. 

    TOKYO - WHILE Koopa attacks (don't wait for him to finish) bulldoze all areas around the fires he starts, so that the fire cannot spread. After he leaves, build new FD's as needed. Once the fires are gone, fix other problems. First priority is ALWAYS to restore power.

    SAN FRANCISCO - After the 1906 earthquake, the 3 FD's you have are not enough. As soon as the scenario starts, PAUSE the game and build FD's, especially in areas where there is none (see the maps). You usually will have 2 months or so between the scenario start and the actual quake. Don't forget to have enough money to keep the FD's funded. When the fires are out, restore power, rezone, and fix the other problems.

    RIO de JANERIO - As soon as this scenario begins, pause it. The coastal flooding won't begin until 2-3 months into the game. Use this time to develop the coastline with zones, parks, etc.... just don't leave any coastline bare. If the water does manage to penetrate the land, build FD's. As always, work on other problems after the flooding is done with.

    DETROIT - The 2 big problems here are high crime and frequent shipwrecks. First off, stop the shipwrecks by building power lines across the river right at the very edges of the map. Although this will keep ships from entering the city, it will not affect industry as long as a seaport is present (yes, you are tricking the game). After the shipwreck problem is out of the way (or you may save it for later), you'll want to stop (or at least lower) the crime rate. Build PD's as needed according to the Crime Rate map and the Police Radius map. Next, try to break up any large clumps of I zones, and finally, place parks in any other open spaces. Parks raise land value, which in turn lowers crime. When crime is lowered to your satisfaction, work on pollution and traffic problems until the scenario ends.

    BERN - When you're faced with major traffic problems, don't go straight to building more roads. Start with the most dense traffic areas and replace the roads with rails. Not only will it eliminate traffic, it eliminates pollution as well! But, remember to keep the new rails "efficient" by connecting them. Don't just build a spotty rail system with few rails here and a few there. This should only take about 2 years, so you'll have plenty of time to work on crime, pollution, etc.

    Bonus Scenarios
    These will only become available when the first six are successfully completed. They appear off the right edge of the screen.

    FREELAND - Since this landform has NO water at all, you have the entire landform to build on, and thus, the best chances of a higher population. There's only 1 catch: it IS considered a scenario, and the game is programmed so that you won't get ANY gifts in scenarios. It's too bad that Freeland had to be included under this restriction, but all the more challenging. Another thing to remember is that even though there is no water, the simulation will still require you to build a seaport at a certain point. The game doesn't check for bodies of water; just whether or not a seaport is in place. Just build it off to a corner somewhere.

    LAS VEGAS - The mother of all scenarios! For those of you who haven't tried it yet, here's a brief description. Las Vegas, with all its casinos, is attacked continously by UFO's. As in most other disasters, this causes fires. Since the UFO's don't come right at the beginning (you'll have up to a year), pause it and build FD's whereever they are needed. If you wait until the UFO's come, it may not be good enough. The UFO's will attack 5 or 6 places each time they come, and they come 5 or 6 times (if anyone gets an exact count, let me know!). All you can do is be ready to bulldoze around the fires. When the fires are out, restore power and rebuild zones. After the UFO's are gone (or if you have time between attacks) try to work on the other problems. One thing is the zig-zag roads. Bulldoze 'em and replace them with tracks in straight lines. You can also eliminate some of the casinos to combat crime, but more PD's are still needed. In the end, you have LOTS of space to make lots of new zones, and/or bulldoze outlying roads to save on transportation expenses. And, notice that somewhere in this waterless city is a seaport, which proves the point addressed about Freeland (above). If you don't believe me, 'doze the seaport and wait until they complain about it.

    Good Luck, Sim-Mayors!  Read on...
    If you don't already have it, I highly recommend SimCity 2000. This game is much more complex than SimCity, as you are responsible for more aspects of the city's running, right down to how much tax is charged to specific industries. Other new items include subways, more power plants choices and arcologies, self-contained cities which can fit thousands of  people on 4 square tile space!
    Unlike SimCity for SNES, SimCity 2000 can save as many games as your hard drive can fit, and it has many more cheats to make many useful and useless things happen. The SimCity Urban Renewal Kit allows you to design your own buildings and SimCopter allows you to fly around your cities in 3-D (actually, SimCopter's great even without SimCity 2000!)
    If you prefer to play games strictly on your SuperNES (or N64) you'll be pleased to know that SimCity 2000 finally made it to the SuperNES late in 1996. I have contacted Nintendo for more details about the game and I hope to be able to post cheats and other info here soon.  As always, rumors are flying that SimCity 2000 or 3000 could be released for the N64, but nothing definite yet.
    SimCity 2000 is also available for the Sega Saturn and Sony Playstation. Unless you have one of these systems, though, you will have to go with the computer version, which is probably the best anyway because you have the resources of this site and many others on the internet to supply cities, tips, cheats and other things that video game systems can't access (yet).
